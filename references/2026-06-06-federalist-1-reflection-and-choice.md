# Federalist No. 1 — "reflection and choice" vs. "accident and force"

*Saved June 6, 2026, as reference material for the Declaration and the essay.*

## The quote

> "It has been frequently remarked," Publius stated, "that it seems to have been reserved to the people of this country . . . to decide the important question, whether societies of men are really capable or not of establishing good government from reflection and choice, or whether they are forever destined to depend for their political constitutions on accident and force."

— Publius (later identified as **Alexander Hamilton**), *The Federalist* No. 1, "General Introduction," published Oct. 27, 1787 in the *Independent Journal*.

## Why it matters for this project

This is, almost word for word, the wager of *We the Users*. The movement's claim is that the people of the internet can establish good digital governance **from reflection and choice** — a deliberately framed constitution, argued in public — rather than remaining forever dependent on the **accident and force** of whatever the incumbents happen to build. Strong candidate epigraph for the Declaration of Internet Independence, or for the opening of the essay.

### Summary

The piece opens on Hamilton's Federalist No. 1 framing — that Americans were uniquely positioned to test whether a people can build government by deliberate choice rather than by force — and reads it alongside the Declaration's natural-rights, human-equality philosophy. It traces the long gap between that theory and the nation's practice (above all slavery), citing William Lloyd Garrison in 1829 and Lincoln at Gettysburg, and argues that the Declaration's principles have endured precisely because they keep calling American constitutional practice back to account.
